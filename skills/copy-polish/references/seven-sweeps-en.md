# The Seven Sweeps — English editorial passes (in depth)

Read this before polishing **English** copy. It is the standalone English half of
`copy-polish`: everything you need to run a voice-preserving edit is here. It expands the
language-neutral model in `shared/references/conversion-principles.md` §2 with detail and
worked before/after pairs — but it does not depend on that file.

The method: make **one focused pass per dimension** instead of fixing everything at once.
Separating the passes keeps each one sharp; a single muddled "make it better" pass fixes
nothing well. (Editorial model credited to Corey Haines' copy-editing work — see the repo
`SOURCES.md`.)

> **Improve, don't rewrite.** The goal of a polish is to sharpen the author's intent, not
> replace their voice. Preserve what works; change only what earns the change.

## 1. First: refresh vs. rewrite — decide explicitly

Before touching the copy, decide which job this is, and **say so to the user**. Stating the
decision prevents the two classic failures: rewriting copy that only needed a polish (and
destroying a working voice), or polishing copy that needed a rethink.

- **Refresh** (default for polishing): the structure and message are sound; tighten
  clarity, voice, proof, specificity, and CTAs in place. Most "make this better" requests
  are refreshes. Do this in place, edit by edit.
- **Rewrite**: the page targets the wrong reader, leads with the wrong message, or has no
  proof to build on. Structure is broken, not just the words. Only rewrite when a refresh
  can't fix the underlying problem — and **flag it first** so the author can choose.

A refresh is reversible edit-by-edit; a rewrite replaces the author's structure. Defaulting
to refresh respects the author. Escalate only with a stated reason.

## 2. Sweep 1 — Clarity

**Question:** Can a scanning reader get the point in one pass?

Readers scan; they do not read. Front-load the point (inverted pyramid): the most important
idea first, in the first line of the section, in plain words. Cut hedges ("we believe we
can help you possibly"), wind-up ("In order to be able to provide…"), and jargon. A clever
line that needs a second read has already lost most of the page.

What to hunt:
- **Wind-up phrases** — "We offer the ability to…", "Our platform is designed to…",
  "It is important to note that…". Delete and start at the verb.
- **Buried lede** — the real point is in sentence three. Move it to sentence one.
- **Hedging** — "can help," "may be able to," "aims to." Commit or cut.
- **Abstraction stacks** — "solutions that drive value across the organization." Say the
  concrete thing.

**Before** → "In order to be able to help organizations improve their security posture, our
solution is designed to provide monitoring capabilities."
**After** → "We monitor your brand around the clock and flag impostors the moment they appear."

## 3. Sweep 2 — Voice & Tone

**Question:** Does it sound like *this brand* — consistently, all the way down?

Check the copy against the brand brief's voice adjectives **and** anti-adjectives. If
there's no brief, infer the voice from the strongest existing lines and from any sample of
the author's own writing, then hold it steady. Keep person (I/we/you) and register (formal
↔ casual) consistent across the whole piece — a hero that's breezy followed by a feature
list that's stiff reads like two writers.

What to hunt:
- **Register drift** — playful headline, bureaucratic body.
- **Person drift** — "we" in one section, "the company" in the next.
- **Borrowed voice** — copy that sounds like a generic SaaS template, not this brand.

Preserve the author's rhythm. If they write short, punchy sentences, don't smooth them into
corporate cadence in the name of "polish." Match their music; don't overwrite it.

**Before** → "Get started in seconds! Subsequently, users may avail themselves of the
configuration interface."
**After** → "Get started in seconds. Then set it up your way — it takes about two minutes."

## 4. Sweep 3 — "So what?"

**Question:** For each claim, *why should the reader care?*

This is the benefits-over-features pass. A feature is *what it is*; a benefit is *what it
does for me*. Don't drop the feature — name it, then land the outcome. If a line states a
capability with no consequence for the reader, it's filler.

What to hunt:
- **Naked features** — "Automated monitoring." So what? → "You hear about a fake shop the
  moment it goes live, not weeks later from an angry customer."
- **Inward claims** — "Built on a modern architecture." The reader doesn't buy architecture;
  they buy what it gets them (speed, reliability, lower cost).
- **Process described, outcome missing** — "We run scans across channels." End on the payoff.

Method: after each sentence, literally ask "so what?" until you hit something the reader
wants. Then write *that*, keeping the feature as the proof.

**Before** → "Our platform offers AI-powered, cross-channel monitoring capabilities."
**After** → "We watch every channel at once and catch impostor accounts automatically —
usually within the hour."

## 5. Sweep 4 — "Prove it"

**Question:** For each claim, *what makes this believable?*

Trust is demonstrated, not asserted. Numbers, named customers, certifications, screenshots,
and concrete guarantees do the work that adjectives like "leading" and "trusted" cannot.
The more a market fears being burned (security, finance, health), the more proof must carry
the copy.

What to hunt:
- **Unbacked superlatives** — "world-class," "best-in-class," "industry-leading." Replace
  with the fact that earns the word, or cut it.
- **Trust asserted, not shown** — "a partner you can rely on" → certification, uptime
  number, response-time guarantee, named logo.
- **Vague scale** — "trusted by many" → "used by 1,200 security teams."

If you can't back a claim, **don't invent proof.** Mark the gap and ask the author for the
real figure. Fabricated numbers are worse than a missing one.

**Before** → "We are a trusted, reliable partner delivering world-class protection."
**After** → "GDPR-compliant, hosted in Germany, incident response within 24 hours,
1,200+ brands protected."

## 6. Sweep 5 — Specificity

**Question:** Could you replace each abstraction with something concrete?

Vague praise is invisible; specifics are memorable and believable. Replace adjectives with
numbers, mechanisms, and named things. This sweep overlaps with "Prove it" but aims wider:
even non-claim copy gets sharper when it's concrete.

Hunt-and-replace targets (the usual suspects): *solutions, powerful, seamless, robust,
world-class, cutting-edge, holistic, end-to-end, next-generation, scalable, streamline,
empower, leverage.* Each is a placeholder for a real thing the author hasn't named yet.

- "fast" → "live in under 5 minutes"
- "powerful reporting" → "export every incident to CSV in one click"
- "seamless integration" → "connect Slack in two clicks; alerts land in your channel"
- "a wide range of threats" → "fake shops, phishing pages, and impostor social profiles"

**Before** → "A powerful, scalable solution that streamlines your security workflow."
**After** → "Catch a fake site, report it, and confirm it's down — all from one dashboard."

## 7. Sweep 6 — Emotion

**Question:** Does the copy connect to a real stake — a fear, a relief, an ambition?

Restraint is fine; flatness is not. Find the human stake behind the transaction and let one
concrete image carry it: the angry customer who got phished, the relief of catching it
first, the founder who can finally stop checking. You don't need hype — you need a true
stake, stated plainly.

What to hunt:
- **All-logic, no-stake copy** — accurate but inert. Add the consequence the reader feels.
- **Manufactured drama** — fear-mongering or exclamation-pile-ups. Pull back to a true,
  specific stake.

Note for B2B and for German: emotion is carried by **concrete stakes and substance**, not
by adjectives or exclamation marks. "Before your customers find the fake shop, you do" beats
"Protect what matters most!" In German B2B especially, hype reads as empty — see
`lektorat-de.md`.

**Before** → "Brand protection is important for modern businesses."
**After** → "By the time a customer emails you about the fake shop, the damage is done. We
catch it first."

## 8. Sweep 7 — Zero risk

**Question:** Is the next step obvious and low-cost?

Every CTA = action verb + benefit, matched to the reader. "Get started" is weaker than
"Start protecting your brand." Lead with a verb, attach the payoff, keep one primary action
per view, and match the page's formality and pronoun. Then lower the perceived cost of
saying yes with a friction-reducer right next to the ask.

What to hunt:
- **Generic CTAs** — "Learn more," "Submit," "Click here." Replace with verb + benefit.
- **Two co-equal CTAs** competing for the click. Pick one primary; demote the other.
- **No friction-reducer** at a big ask — add "No credit card," "Cancel anytime," "Free for
  30 days," "2-minute setup." The bigger the commitment, the more this matters.

**Before** → "Learn more" / "Submit"
**After** → "Start protecting your brand — no credit card" / "Book a 20-minute demo"

## 9. Named anti-patterns

Recognize these on sight; each has a standard fix.

- **Wall of Features** — a list of capabilities with no outcomes. *Fix:* lead each with the
  benefit; group features under the job they do for the reader.
- **Corporate Speak** — abstract, hedged, committee-written prose that says nothing a
  competitor couldn't say. *Fix:* specifics, plain verbs, a human voice. (German variant:
  Floskeln / Worthülsen — see `lektorat-de.md`.)
- **Buried CTA** — the reader is sold but can't find or doesn't understand the next step.
  *Fix:* one clear, visible, verb-led primary action.
- **No Proof** — claims with nothing behind them. *Fix:* numbers, names, certifications,
  guarantees — or cut the claim.
- **Me-First** — copy about the company ("We are…", "Our mission…") instead of the reader's
  outcome. *Fix:* turn the lens around; lead with what the reader gets. A fast diagnostic:
  count "we/our" vs. "you/your" in the hero. If "we" wins, it's Me-First.

## 10. The competitor copy-paste test

The fastest filler-detector in this file: **could a competitor paste this exact sentence
onto their own site without it becoming false?** If yes, the sentence says nothing specific
to you — replace it with a number, a named capability, a mechanism, or a proof point.

Apply it to every hero line, value prop, and trust statement. It works identically in
English and German, and it's the single most useful question you can ask while polishing.

- "We deliver world-class solutions for businesses of all sizes." → any company could paste
  this → **fails** → rewrite with specifics.
- "We take down 1,200+ fake shops a month, with confirmation within 24 hours." → only true
  for you → **passes** → keep.

## 11. Quick checklist

- [ ] Stated the verdict: **refresh** or **rewrite** (and flagged a rewrite before doing it).
- [ ] Read the brand brief (or inferred and held the voice); respected banned words/glossary.
- [ ] Ran each sweep once: Clarity → Voice → So what? → Prove it → Specificity → Emotion →
      Zero risk.
- [ ] Every hero line, value prop, and trust statement passes the competitor copy-paste test.
- [ ] No invented proof; flagged gaps for the author to fill.
- [ ] Preserved the author's rhythm and any working headline.
- [ ] Delivered: verdict + clean copy + a short "what changed and why" keyed to the sweeps.
