# Landing-page structure — section by section (bilingual)

The section-by-section spine of a landing or sales page: what each section is *for*, the
order, and what "good" looks like. Read this **before planning a page**. It is
self-contained — you do not need any other file to plan a structure.

All examples are **original and constructed** (domain: brand protection / anti-phishing,
B2B security) for illustration. None are copied from any real site. German execution detail
lives in `landing-de-notes.md`; this file gives the bilingual notes inline.

## 1. The three laws

A page is one argument, not a stack of sections. Three laws keep it coherent:

- **One primary action, repeated.** The whole page asks for *one* thing. Every CTA points
  at that same action; repeating it (hero, mid-page, final) raises conversion. A quieter
  secondary link ("See pricing") is fine — a second equal-weight CTA is an error, because
  it splits the reader's decision.
- **One idea per section.** Each section makes a single point. Two points → split it. No
  point → cut it. This is what lets the ~80% of readers who only scan get the argument from
  the section headings alone.
- **Front-load every section.** The point goes in the first line, in plain words (inverted
  pyramid). Readers decide whether to continue at the *top* of each section. A clever line
  that needs a second read has already lost the scanner.

The fastest filler-detector, applied to every line: **could a competitor paste this exact
sentence onto their site without it becoming false?** If yes, replace it with a number, a
named capability, a mechanism, or a proof point.

## 2. The default section order

| # | Section | The one job |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Hero | State the one promise + the primary CTA, above the fold. |
| 2 | Social proof strip | Buy a second look with logos or one number. |
| 3 | Problem / agitation | Name the reader's stake so the solution matters. |
| 4 | Solution / how it works | Turn the mechanism into a simple path (≈3 steps). |
| 5 | Features → benefits | Convert each capability into the reader's outcome. |
| 6 | Proof | Make the claims believable (cases, metrics, testimonials). |
| 7 | Objection handling / FAQ | Remove the last reasons a ready buyer hesitates. |
| 8 | Trust line | Checkable facts, placed next to the ask. |
| 9 | Final CTA | Restate the one action with a friction-reducer. |

Adapt to the offer (see §12). The near-universal spine is **hero → proof → solution →
proof → ask**. You may cut or fuse sections; do not reorder so the point ends up buried.

## 3. Section 1 — Hero

**Job:** make one promise and offer the one action, before the reader scrolls.

**Contains:** benefit headline (one promise) + subhead (mechanism / for-whom / proof, one
scannable line) + primary CTA. Optionally a single proof chip ("1,200+ brands").

**What good looks like:**
- The headline carries the **differentiator**, not a category label. "Brand protection
  software" is a label; "Fake shops abusing your brand, taken down — not just reported"
  is a promise only this brand can make.
- The subhead extends the headline — it does **not** repeat it. It answers *how / for whom
  / prove it*.
- The CTA is the page's one action, verb-led, with a friction-reducer nearby.

**Avoid:** two promises fighting in one headline; adjective stacks ("innovative, end-to-end,
holistic"); a clever line that needs decoding.

**Constructed example (EN):**
> **Fake shops abusing your brand, taken down — not just reported.**
> We detect the impersonation, file the takedown, and confirm when it's gone.
> 1,200+ brands protected · response within 24 hours.
> CTA: **Book a demo**

**German note:** write the hero native (see `landing-de-notes.md` §Hero). German prefers
restraint over hype — concrete outcome + mechanism beats stacked superlatives. Native CTA
(„Demo vereinbaren“, not „Buche eine Demo“). Lock du/Sie here; it governs the whole page.

---

## 4. Section 2 — Social proof strip

**Job:** in one glance, signal "others like you trust this," so the reader invests another
ten seconds. Placed immediately under the hero.

**What good looks like:** a row of recognizable customer logos, or a single hard number
("Protecting 1,200+ brands across DACH"), or a short trust badge row (certifications). One
of these, not all three competing.

**Avoid:** vague claims ("trusted by many", "thousands of happy customers") — they are
adjectives, not proof. If you have no logos or numbers yet, use a real third-party signal
(a named certification, an integration partner) or omit the strip rather than fake it.

**Constructed example (EN):** "Trusted by security teams at [Logo] [Logo] [Logo] — 1,200+
brands monitored."

**German note:** „Über 1.200 Marken geschützt“ — note the German thousands separator
(`1.200`, not `1,200`). Keep it factual; no „führende Unternehmen vertrauen uns“ Floskel.

---

## 5. Section 3 — Problem / agitation

**Job:** name the reader's problem in *their* words and raise the stake, so the solution
that follows feels necessary rather than nice-to-have. This is the empathy beat.

**What good looks like:**
- Mirror the voice of the customer — the words they use in sales calls and tickets, not
  internal jargon.
- Make the cost concrete and emotional-but-restrained: *what goes wrong, and who it hurts*.
- One problem framed well beats three listed shallowly.

**Avoid:** melodrama or fear-mongering (especially in B2B and in German, where hype reads
as untrustworthy); listing problems the product doesn't actually solve.

**Constructed example (EN):**
> You find out about the fake shop the way your customers do — after someone's already been
> scammed with your logo on the receipt. By the time support escalates it, the clone has
> been live for days, and the takedown crawls through a platform form nobody answers.

**German note:** carry the stake through **concrete detail and Verbalstil**, not abstract
Substantivierungen. „Der Schaden entsteht durch die verspätete Erkennung gefälschter
Seiten“ (Nominalstil, cold) → „Sie erfahren von der Fake-Seite zu spät – meist erst, wenn
ein Kunde sich beschwert“ (verbal, warm). Restraint over alarm.

---

## 6. Section 4 — Solution / how it works

**Job:** show that the path from problem to outcome is simple and proven. Usually **three
steps** — enough to feel concrete, few enough to feel easy.

**What good looks like:**
- A clear 3-step mechanism with active verbs: *Detect → Report → Confirm*.
- Each step is a verb + outcome, not a feature dump.
- The reader should finish thinking "that's it?" — simplicity is the selling point.

**Avoid:** more than ~4 steps (it stops looking easy); passive constructions that hide who
does the work ("the impersonation is detected" — by whom?).

**Constructed example (EN):**
> **1. Detect.** We monitor the web and social platforms and flag impersonations
> automatically — usually within the hour.
> **2. Report.** Our team files the takedown with the right registrar or platform.
> **3. Confirm.** You get notified — with proof — the moment the fake is offline.

**German note:** keep the steps **active and verbal** (§C/§D in `landing-de-notes.md`):
„Wir erkennen … Wir melden … Wir bestätigen.“ Active voice matters doubly for a security
brand — passive hides the actor, and the actor (you, doing the work) is the whole pitch.

---

## 7. Section 5 — Features → benefits

**Job:** convert each capability into the reader's outcome. This is where most pages drop
into a "Wall of Features"; resist it.

**What good looks like:** for each feature, write the chain **feature → so what → proof**
and **lead with the middle link**:
- *Feature:* what it is. *Benefit:* what it does for me. *Proof:* why I believe it.

Lead with the benefit (a scannable promise, not a label — "Detection" is a label; "Know
within the hour" is a benefit), name the feature, attach proof. Group features under the
**job** they do; three strong blocks beat eight thin ones.

**Avoid:** a bullet list of nouns; "AI-powered", "robust", "seamless" doing the work a
concrete number should do.

**Constructed example (EN):**
> **Know the moment someone clones your brand.** We watch the web and social around the
> clock and flag impersonations automatically — usually within the hour. *(feature:
> 24/7 automated monitoring; proof: the speed.)*

**German note:** „Nutzen statt Funktion“ is the core rule. Lead with the outcome, name the
feature, then the proof. Avoid empty „leistungsstark/innovativ“ adjectives — replace each
with a fact (§H in `landing-de-notes.md`). Keep English tech terms that are genuinely
established with the right article (das Dashboard, das Monitoring).

---

## 8. Section 6 — Proof

**Job:** make the claims believable. Trust is demonstrated, not asserted — and the more the
market fears being burned (security, finance, health), the more weight this section carries.

**What good looks like:** a case study with a **before/after number**, hard metrics, and/or
named testimonials with a real person, role, and company. A specific result ("cut active
impersonations by 70% in 6 weeks") beats any adjective.

**Avoid:** anonymous quotes ("— a happy customer"); metrics with no baseline; anything you'd
have to invent. If proof is thin, use one real, checkable fact rather than three vague ones,
and placehold the rest with `[ ]` for the user to fill.

**Constructed example (EN):**
> "Before [Product], we were finding clones weeks late. Now we hear within the hour and
> most are down inside two days." — [Name], Head of Security, [Company].
> *[Customer] cut active impersonations by [X]% in [N] weeks.*

**German note:** echte Kundenzitate sind das stärkste Trust-Signal im deutschen B2B (§H).
Numbers use German formatting (Prozent with a space: „70 %“; „1.200“). No invented quotes.

---

## 9. Section 7 — Objection handling / FAQ

**Job:** answer the real reasons a reader who is *almost* convinced still hesitates. A good
FAQ is sales, not support — each question removes a specific blocker to acting.

**What good looks like:**
- Questions phrased the way a skeptical buyer actually asks ("How fast is a takedown
  really?", "What if it flags a legitimate reseller?", "Do you cover social media too?").
- Short, direct, honest answers. Hedging here costs trust.
- Order by how often the objection actually blocks the sale.

**Avoid:** softball questions that exist to repeat marketing claims ("Why is [Product] so
great?"); long essays where a sentence will do.

**German note:** answer in plain, active German; avoid Behördington and aufgeblähte
Höflichkeit ("Wir möchten Sie darauf hinweisen, dass…"). du/Sie consistent with the page.

---

## 10. Section 8 — Trust line

**Job:** place the checkable, risk-reducing facts right where the reader is deciding —
typically just above or beside the final CTA.

**What good looks like:** a tight row of verifiable facts: certifications, data location,
response time, scale, guarantee. Each is a fact a competitor could **not** truthfully
copy, or it doesn't belong.

**Constructed example (EN):** "GDPR-compliant · data hosted in Germany · response within
24 hours · 1,200+ brands protected · cancel anytime."

**German note:** draw from the Trust-Vokabular (because it's checkable): DSGVO-konform,
Serverstandort Deutschland, SSL-verschlüsselt, Antwort in 24 Stunden, fester
Ansprechpartner, transparente Preise, Made in Germany, geprüft, revisionssicher. Typography:
„24 Stunden“, „1.200+“, currency/percent rules from `landing-de-notes.md`.

---

## 11. Section 9 — Final CTA

**Job:** restate the one action for the reader who scrolled the whole way, and make saying
yes feel cheap.

**What good looks like:** the same primary action as the hero (verb + benefit), a
**friction-reducer** beside it ("No credit card", „Keine Kreditkarte nötig“, "20 minutes,
no prep"), and ideally a one-line restatement of the core promise so the button has context.

**Avoid:** introducing a *new* action here (splits the decision at the worst moment); a bare
"Submit" / „Absenden“ that hides what happens next.

**Constructed example (EN):**
> **See your brand's exposure in a 20-minute demo.**
> CTA: **Book a demo** · *No prep needed. We'll show you live what's out there.*

**German note:** native CTA + Friction-Reducer, du/Sie matched: „Demo vereinbaren · Keine
Kreditkarte nötig“ or „Jederzeit kündbar“. One primary action — same as the hero.

## 12. Adapting the structure

The nine sections are a default, not a mold. Adapt to the offer:

- **Short page / simple offer:** fuse Problem + Solution, drop the FAQ, keep hero → proof →
  features → trust → CTA. One idea per section still holds.
- **High-trust / high-price (security, finance, health):** weight the Proof and Trust
  sections heavily and repeat trust signals; the market's fear of being burned means proof
  carries the page.
- **Cold traffic (problem-aware):** the reader feels the pain but doesn't know solutions
  exist — spend more on Problem/agitation and "How it works"; don't rush the ask.
- **Warm traffic (solution-aware):** they're comparing approaches or vendors; compress the
  problem to a nod — lead with the differentiator and proof.
- **Branded traffic (product-aware):** they know *you* and came to decide; weight FAQ,
  trust line, and friction-reducers, and put the ask high. Matching the page to the
  reader's awareness stage is the same move `seo-copy` makes with search intent.
- **Long sales page:** repeat the CTA after every 1–2 sections, always the same action,
  always with a reason to click now.

What you never do: reorder so the main point or the proof ends up below the fold of the
reader's patience, add a second competing primary CTA, or pad with a section that makes no
single point. Cut freely; keep the spine.
