# English headlines & CTAs — formulas, patterns, do/don't

Read this when writing English headlines, hero lines, subheads, or button copy. It is the
execution layer for English; the language-neutral craft lives in `SKILL.md`. Everything
here is original and constructed — no scraped copy. Examples use a brand-protection /
anti-phishing B2B context, but the patterns transfer to any offer.

---

## 1. The headline job

A headline has one job: earn the next line. The reader is scanning, deciding in a second
whether to stay. So:

- **Lead with the benefit, name the feature second.** The reader cares what they get, not
  what you built. Then ground the benefit in the mechanism so it's believable.
- **Make one promise.** A headline that carries two ideas splits the reader's attention.
  Move the second idea into the subhead.
- **Be specific.** A number, a named capability, or a concrete mechanism beats any
  adjective. "Powerful protection" says nothing; "Takedowns confirmed in under 24 hours"
  says everything.
- **Clarity over cleverness.** Wordplay that needs a second read has already lost the
  80 % who only scan. Save cleverness for when meaning survives without it.

**The competitor copy-paste test:** could a competitor paste your headline onto their site
without it becoming false? If yes, it's filler — replace it with something only you can say.

## 2. Headline formulas (scaffolding, not crutches)

Formulas get you a draft fast. Once the idea is clear, drop the scaffold so the line reads
like a sentence, not a template.

**Benefit-first / outcome lead** — the default.
- Template: `[Outcome the reader gets] + [mechanism that proves it].`
- "Fake sites impersonating your brand, offline — usually within hours."

**PAS (Problem · Agitate · Solution)** — for pain-aware readers; great for hero + subhead.
- Headline names the problem and the relief; subhead agitates and resolves.
- Headline: "Your customers can't tell your site from a phishing clone."
  Subhead: "Neither can their inbox filters. We find the clones and get them taken down
  before the first victim clicks."

**AIDA (Attention · Interest · Desire · Action)** — for a full section, not one line.
- Attention = headline; Interest = subhead; Desire = proof block; Action = CTA. Use it to
  structure a hero, not to cram four jobs into one sentence.

**The "how" / outcome-without-the-work** pattern.
- Template: `[Result] without [the dreaded effort].`
- "Brand monitoring without a full-time analyst watching dashboards."

**Number / specificity lead.**
- Template: `[Number] + [unit of value].`
- "1,200 brands. Zero copy-and-paste clones left standing."

**Contrast / before-after.**
- Template: `Less [pain]. More [gain].`
- "Less time chasing fakes. More time on real threats."

**Question that names the reader's stake** — use sparingly; never a question the reader
can answer "no" and leave.
- "Know within the hour when someone clones your brand?"

## 3. Hero-line patterns

The hero is headline + subhead + one primary CTA. Patterns that consistently read well:

- **Outcome + mechanism:** "We find the fake sites abusing your brand — and get them taken
  down." (verb-led, concrete threats, company recedes)
- **Segment + plain noun:** "Brand protection for security teams who are tired of playing
  whack-a-mole." (names the reader and the real job)
- **Stakes-led:** "Stop brand impersonation before your customers get burned." (leads with
  the fear that actually moves a security buyer)
- **Proof-led:** "The takedown service 1,200 brands trust to clear phishing clones fast."
  (only if the number is real)

Subhead rules: it expands the headline's one promise, adds the mechanism or proof, and
sets up the CTA. Keep it to one or two sentences. Don't repeat the headline in other words.

## 4. Native English CTAs

Lead with a verb, attach the payoff, match the page's formality, and keep one primary
action per view. A second, quieter CTA (a "ghost" button) is fine for the not-yet-ready.

| Job | Native CTA examples |
|---|---|
| Begin / sign up | "Start free" · "Create your account" · "Start protecting your brand" |
| Trial | "Start a free trial" · "Try it free for 14 days" |
| Demo | "Book a demo" · "Book a 20-minute demo" · "See it in action" |
| Quote / sales | "Get a quote" · "Talk to sales" · "Get pricing" |
| Low-commitment / scan | "Scan my brand now" · "Check my exposure" |
| Download | "Get the guide" · "Download the report" |

Always pair the primary CTA with a **friction-reducer**: "No credit card," "Cancel
anytime," "Free for 30 days," "2-minute setup," "No sales call." The bigger the ask, the
more it matters.

## 5. Do / don't pairs

| ✗ Don't | ✓ Do | Why |
|---|---|---|
| "Learn more" | "See how takedowns work" | Says what the reader gets, not a generic verb. |
| "Submit" | "Send my request" / "Get my report" | "Submit" describes the form, not the payoff. |
| "Click here" | (verb-led label on the action itself) | "Click here" is a relic; the link text should name the action. |
| "Empowering brands to safeguard their integrity" | "We get fake sites impersonating you taken down." | Cut puffery; lead with the concrete outcome. |
| "Our innovative, world-class platform" | "Takedowns confirmed in under 24 hours." | Replace adjectives with a checkable fact. |
| "Solutions for all your brand security needs" | "Find phishing clones and get them offline." | "Solutions … needs" is filler a rival could reuse. |
| "Fast, simple, and powerful" | Pick the one that's true and prove it. | Rule-of-three on autopilot reads like AI; one proven claim beats three vague ones. |
| Two promises in one headline | Headline = promise 1; subhead = promise 2 | One idea per line keeps the reader. |
| "Make a request" (German-thought English) | "Get a quote" | Native English, not a rendered phrase. |

---

## 6. Quick self-check before you ship

- Benefit leads; the feature is named, not buried or dropped.
- Exactly one promise in the headline.
- At least one specific (number, name, mechanism) instead of an adjective.
- CTA is verb + benefit, with a friction-reducer.
- The headline fails the competitor copy-paste test for a *rival* (i.e. it's true only of
  this brand).
- No AI-tell adjectives ("world-class," "cutting-edge," "seamless," "robust"). If polishing
  AI-flavored drafts, see the `humanizer-en` skill.
