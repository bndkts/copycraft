# Choosing voice dimensions

How to turn "we want to sound professional" into a voice that actually guides sentences.
Language-neutral — the method works identically in English and German; only the example
wording changes. Read this when the user's voice words are vague or they want help picking.

## Contents
1. Voice vs. tone (the distinction to get right first)
2. Why pairs, not lists
3. The menu of common dimensions
4. How to run the choice
5. The sample-sentence test

---

## 1. Voice vs. tone

- **Voice is constant.** It's the brand's personality — the same on a pricing page, a
  404 error, and a launch announcement. You set it once in the brief.
- **Tone shifts by context.** The *same* voice gets quieter in an outage notice and warmer
  in a welcome email. Tone is voice adjusted for the reader's situation.

Record both: the 3–5 voice adjectives (constant) and a note on how tone flexes (error vs.
celebration vs. sales). A brand with one flat tone everywhere sounds robotic; a brand with
no constant voice sounds like a committee.

---

## 2. Why pairs, not lists

A single adjective ("professional") is unfalsifiable — nobody picks *unprofessional*, so it
guides nothing. A **pair** names the trade-off and draws the line:

> "Warm, **not** formal" tells a writer to choose contractions and direct address.
> "Confident, **not** hypey" tells them to state results without superlatives.

So capture **3–5 adjectives plus 1–2 anti-adjectives**. The anti-adjectives are where the
voice gets its edges. "We're warm but never cutesy" is far more useful than five positive
words in a row.

---

## 3. The menu of common dimensions

Pick the side of each pair that fits — or mark a dimension "neutral" if it doesn't matter
for this brand. You don't need all of them; 3–5 chosen dimensions make a voice.

| Dimension | One end | Other end | What it controls |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warmth | **warm** (you, we, contractions, empathy) | **formal** (distance, third person, restraint) | how close the brand stands to the reader |
| Plainness | **plain** (short words, everyday language) | **technical** (precise domain terms, specs) | how much expertise the copy assumes |
| Assertiveness | **bold** (strong claims, opinion) | **measured** (careful, qualified, evidence-led) | how much the brand pushes vs. lets facts speak |
| Energy | **calm** (steady, reassuring) | **urgent** (fast, action-driven, FOMO) | the emotional tempo |
| Playfulness | **playful** (humor, wordplay, wink) | **serious** (straight, no jokes) | whether wit is on the table |
| Register | **casual** (spoken, loose) | **polished** (tight, edited, exact) | how finished the prose feels |

Notes that keep choices honest:

- **Warm ≠ casual, and formal ≠ cold.** You can be warm *and* polished (precise but human).
  In German specifically, warmth comes from phrasing, not from the pronoun — you can be warm
  with *Sie* and stiff with *du*. (Set the du/Sie choice in the brief; it's separate from voice.)
- **Trust-sensitive markets** (security, finance, health) usually lean **calm + measured**:
  hype reads as risk. Energy and bold claims belong to lower-stakes, consumer-y brands.
- **Plain almost always wins for clarity** — but a brand selling to specialists earns the
  right to be technical when the precise term *is* the clearer word for that reader.

---

## 4. How to run the choice

1. Take the user's first words ("professional, trustworthy") and name them as a starting
   point, not an answer — they're too generic to guide a sentence.
2. Walk 3–4 dimensions from the menu as either/or questions: "Warmer or more formal?
   Plain or technical? Calm or urgent?"
3. Land **3–5 adjectives + 1–2 anti-adjectives**. Force at least one anti-adjective — it's
   where the voice gets defined.
4. Confirm with the sample-sentence test below before writing it into the brief.

---

## 5. The sample-sentence test

Prove the voice by writing the *same* line two ways and checking which one the user wants
to live next to. Make it a real surface for their brand (a status line, a CTA, a headline).

> **Off-voice (generic / hypey):** "Experience world-class protection, trusted by leaders."
> **On-voice (calm, plain, specific):** "We found 14 fake shops using your logo last month
> and took down 12."

If the user points at the second line, you've confirmed *calm, plain, specific, proof-led* —
and you've also confirmed the anti-adjectives (*hypey, vague*). Record the winning line in
the brief as a touchstone; downstream skills can pattern-match against it.

A voice you can't demonstrate in one sentence isn't specific enough yet. Keep narrowing the
pairs until the on-voice line is obviously different from the off-voice one.
