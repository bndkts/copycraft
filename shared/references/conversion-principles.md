# Conversion Copy Principles (language-neutral)

The shared craft layer cited by the writing and polishing skills in both languages
(`copywriting-en`, `texten-de`, `copy-polish`, `landing-page`, `seo-copy`,
`headlines-and-ctas`). These principles are language-independent. The *execution*
is not: how you phrase a benefit, a CTA, or proof differs by language, so always
combine these principles with the language-specific references (for German, the
`lektorat-de`, `typografie-de`, `du-sie-check` rules and the native CTA map).

> These are principles, not hard rules. Apply judgment. When a principle and the
> brand brief disagree, the brand brief wins.

## 1. The seven core principles

1. **Benefits over features — but earn it with the feature.** Translate every
   feature into the outcome the reader gets ("Nutzen statt Funktion"). A feature is
   *what it is*; a benefit is *what it does for me*. Don't drop the feature — name it,
   then land the outcome. "Automated monitoring" → "You hear about a fake shop the
   moment it goes live, not weeks later."

2. **Mirror the voice of the customer.** Use the audience's own words, pulled from
   reviews, sales calls, and support tickets — not internal jargon. Copy that uses
   the reader's vocabulary feels like it was written *for* them. This is the single
   cheapest way to sound relevant.

3. **Clarity beats cleverness.** Readers scan; they do not read. Front-load the point
   (inverted pyramid): the most important idea first, in the first line of the section,
   in plain words. A clever headline that needs a second read has already lost most of
   the page. If a sentence makes the reader work, the reader leaves.

4. **One idea per section; specific beats vague.** Each section earns its place by
   making one point. Replace adjectives with numbers, mechanisms, and proof:
   "fast" → "live in under 5 minutes"; "trusted by many" → "used by 1,200 security teams."
   Vague praise is invisible; specifics are memorable and believable.

5. **Show proof, don't claim trust.** Trust is demonstrated, not asserted. Numbers,
   named customers, certifications, screenshots, and concrete guarantees do the work
   that adjectives like "leading" and "trusted" cannot. The more a market fears being
   burned (security, finance, health), the more proof must carry the copy.

6. **Every CTA = action verb + benefit, matched to the reader.** "Get started" is
   weaker than "Start protecting your brand." Lead with a verb, attach the payoff, and
   match the formality/pronoun the rest of the page uses. One primary action per view.

7. **Reduce risk at the point of action.** Next to every ask, lower the perceived cost
   of saying yes: "No credit card," "Cancel anytime," "Free for 30 days," "2-minute setup."
   The bigger the commitment, the more the friction-reducer matters.

## 2. The "Seven Sweeps" editorial passes (for polishing existing copy)

When *improving* copy rather than generating it, make one focused pass per dimension.
Separating the passes keeps each one sharp; trying to fix everything at once fixes
nothing well. (Model: Corey Haines' `copy-editing` skill — see SOURCES.md.)

1. **Clarity** — Can a scanning reader get the point in one pass? Cut hedges, jargon,
   and wind-up. Front-load.
2. **Voice & tone** — Does it sound like the brand brief (adjectives *and* anti-adjectives)?
   Consistent person and register throughout.
3. **"So what?"** — For each claim, ask *why the reader should care*. If there's no
   answer, the line is filler. Make the benefit explicit.
4. **"Prove it"** — For each claim, ask *what makes this believable*. Add the number,
   the name, the mechanism, the guarantee. Remove claims you can't back.
5. **Specificity** — Replace abstractions and adjectives with concrete detail. Hunt
   for "solutions," "powerful," "seamless," "world-class" and make them say something.
6. **Emotion** — Does the copy connect to a real stake — a fear, a relief, an ambition?
   Restraint is fine; flatness is not. (In German B2B, emotion is carried by concrete
   stakes and Substanz, not by hype — see `lektorat-de`.)
7. **Zero risk** — Is the next step obvious and low-cost? Strengthen the CTA and add a
   friction-reducer.

**Improve, don't rewrite.** The goal of a polish is to sharpen the author's intent,
not to replace their voice. Preserve what works; change only what earns the change.

## 3. Refresh vs. rewrite — decide explicitly

Before touching the copy, decide which job this is, and say so:

- **Refresh** (default for polishing): the structure and message are sound; tighten
  clarity, voice, proof, specificity, and CTAs in place. Most "make this better"
  requests are refreshes.
- **Rewrite**: the page targets the wrong reader, leads with the wrong message, or has
  no proof to build on. Structure is broken, not just the words. Only rewrite when a
  refresh can't fix the underlying problem — and flag it to the user first.

Stating the decision prevents the two classic failures: rewriting copy that only needed
a polish (and destroying a working voice), or polishing copy that needed a rethink.

## 4. Named anti-patterns to recognize

- **Wall of Features** — a list of capabilities with no outcomes. Fix: lead each with
  the benefit; group features under the job they do.
- **Corporate Speak** — abstract, hedged, committee-written prose that says nothing a
  competitor couldn't say. Fix: specifics, plain verbs, a human voice. (German variant:
  Floskeln / Worthülsen — see `lektorat-de`.)
- **Buried CTA** — the reader is sold but can't find or doesn't understand the next step.
  Fix: one clear primary action, visible, verb-led.
- **No Proof** — claims with nothing behind them. Fix: numbers, names, certifications,
  guarantees — or cut the claim.
- **Me-First** — copy about the company ("We are…", "Our mission…") instead of the
  reader's outcome. Fix: turn the lens around; lead with what the reader gets.

## 5. The competitor copy-paste test

The fastest filler-detector: **could a competitor paste this exact sentence onto their
own site without it becoming false?** If yes, the sentence says nothing specific to you —
replace it with a number, a named capability, a mechanism, or a proof point. Apply it to
every hero line, value prop, and trust statement. It is the single most useful question
in this file, and it works identically in English and German.
